# Cultural Identity in Richmond's West End and Policy Avenues for Mobilizing Care for the Inner City

## The research question

How is cultural identity constructed in Richmond, Virginia's West End, and what avenues can policymakers use to redirect West End civic energy toward care for the city's inner-city neighborhoods?

The inquiry covers the local umbrella called the West End — the Fan, Museum District, Carytown, Scott's Addition, the Near West End / Westhampton, and the [University of Richmond](https://engage.richmond.edu/) campus border — set against East End and South Side historically Black neighborhoods and the city's six original public-housing communities. The period runs from HOLC redlining and mid-century urban renewal through 2020–2026 memorial politics and Mayor Danny Avula's housing agenda.

Key definitions used in this packet:

| Term | Working definition | Disputed? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| West End (Richmond) | Folk umbrella for the affluent western corridor, not a single official neighborhood boundary ([Virginia Places](http://www.virginiaplaces.org/vacities/24richwestend.html); [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_End_(Richmond,_Virginia))) | Yes — boundaries overlap and locals disagree |
| Inner city / East End | Historically Black neighborhoods and public-housing concentrations carrying redlining, urban renewal, and highway displacement ([The Valentine](https://thevalentine.org/explore/exhibitions/online-exhibitions/racial-equity-health-series/race-space-and-power-in-richmond-virginia/); [RRHA Communities](https://www.rrha.com/housing/communities)) | No as geography; yes as political framing |
| Changing hearts and minds | Redirecting West End civic, philanthropic, and political energy so East End distress is treated as a shared city problem — narrative plus structural shared-risk policy | Yes — soft rhetoric vs. hard policy |

Populations in scope include West End residents, East End and public-housing residents, Richmond Public Schools students, named anchor institutions (UR, VCU, Virginia Union, Bon Secours, VCU Health), and city leadership. The research does not include original resident surveys or a full county fiscal-incidence audit.

## Executive summary

Richmond is spatially polarized. The West End corridor is majority-white, college-educated, and comparatively wealthy; East End and South Side neighborhoods — Jackson Ward, Gilpin Court, Highland Park, Whitcomb, Creighton, Hillside, and Mosby Courts among them — are disproportionately Black and low-income and carry the cumulative weight of redlining, urban renewal, and public-housing concentration ([The Valentine](https://thevalentine.org/explore/exhibitions/online-exhibitions/racial-equity-health-series/race-space-and-power-in-richmond-virginia/); [HOME of VA redlining booklet](https://homeofva.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/06/RIR_Booklet_HUD_10_26_18_Web.pdf)).

West End cultural identity is real but layered: historic-preservation pride (including Monument Avenue's contested memorial landscape), creative-economy vitality in Carytown and Scott's Addition, university presence, and geographic separation from the Black urban core ([Fairfield Foundation on Monument Avenue](https://fairfieldfoundation.org/witness-to-change-the-rise-and-fall-of-monument-avenues-confederate-statues); [Virginia Places](http://www.virginiaplaces.org/vacities/24richwestend.html)).

The research argues that durable "hearts and minds" change requires five paired avenues: place-based narrative reframing; sustained equal-status contact; anchor-institution leverage; zoning and fiscal policy that puts West End and East End households in shared risk pools; and democratic reforms that tie both halves into one electorate. Live vehicles include Mayor Avula's [More Homes for More People](https://rva.gov/mayors-office/more-homes-more-people) plan and existing nonprofits and civic-engagement programs — alongside the December 2025 mayoral rejection of the then-current [Gilpin Court](https://www.facebook.com/CBS6News/posts/richmond-mayor-danny-avula-said-he-cannot-support-the-current-plan-to-redevelop-/1337485221297352) redevelopment plan over displacement concerns.

Honesty check: several structural claims (tax-base capture magnitude; West End attitude change from contact programs; comparative Virginia municipal revenue authority) are thinly evidenced or are model synthesis. Historical displacement and HOLC geography are stronger.

## Major findings

### Finding 1 — West End identity is layered, not monolithic (moderate, descriptive)

Locals use "West End" for overlapping areas: streetcar-suburb Fan and Museum District (majority white; Fan median household income often cited around $70,000–$90,000), Carytown and Scott's Addition as retail/creative corridors, Near West End / Westhampton as a high-price single-family enclave (home prices routinely above $700,000; median incomes above $120,000 in place profiles), and the University of Richmond's roughly 4,000-undergraduate campus as a physical and psychological border ([Virginia Places](http://www.virginiaplaces.org/vacities/24richwestend.html); [Wikipedia West End](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_End_(Richmond,_Virginia)); [Bonner Center](https://engage.richmond.edu/)).

Monument Avenue anchors the symbolic geography: planned as a Confederate memorial corridor, cleared of those statues after 2020, with the Arthur Ashe monument installed in 2021 as the first statue on the avenue honoring a Black Virginian ([Fairfield Foundation](https://fairfieldfoundation.org/witness-to-change-the-rise-and-fall-of-monument-avenues-confederate-statues); [VPM coverage of ongoing memorial debates](https://www.vpm.org/news/2025-07-11/virginia-impacts-confederate-monuments-trump-stoney-martin-valentine)). Identity here is pride in landscape and culture plus unresolved argument over what the corridor should teach.

### Finding 2 — Inner-city disparities track a documented disinvestment arc (strong, causal-historical)

Jackson Ward, once called the "Harlem of the South," was cut by Interstate 95 after World War II; federal highway routing displaced more than 7,000 residents — about 10 percent of Richmond's Black population at the time — and razed hundreds of Black-owned homes and businesses ([The Valentine, Race, Space and Power](https://thevalentine.org/explore/exhibitions/online-exhibitions/racial-equity-health-series/race-space-and-power-in-richmond-virginia/); [Redlining Virginia](https://redliningvirginia.org/)).

In the 1930s, HOLC maps graded most Black neighborhoods "D – Hazardous" and the white West End "A – Best" or "B – Still Desirable"; federal mortgage insurance largely followed those grades ([Mapping Inequality](https://dsl.richmond.edu/panorama/redlining/introduction); [HOME of VA booklet](https://homeofva.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/06/RIR_Booklet_HUD_10_26_18_Web.pdf)).

Six original public-housing developments — Gilpin, Hillside, Creighton, Whitcomb, Mosby, and Highland Park (Fairfield) — built 1942–1961 concentrated Black residents displaced by slum clearance; poverty rates in these communities run several times the citywide average ([RRHA Communities](https://www.rrha.com/housing/communities); [Library of Virginia public-housing history](https://uncommonwealth.lva.virginia.gov/blog/2025/11/17/a-brief-history-of-public-housing-in-the-city-of-richmond)).

### Finding 3 — Contemporary gaps remain large across income, schools, wealth, and life expectancy (moderate, descriptive)

Citywide, Richmond is roughly 41 percent white, 41 percent Black, and 16 percent Hispanic or Latino; median household income about $64,587 (2019–2023 ACS) with poverty near 18 percent ([World Population Review compilation of Census/ACS](https://worldpopulationreview.com/us-cities/virginia/richmond)). East End tracts such as Census Tract 204 lag the city median by tens of thousands of dollars; West End tracts run far higher ([Data Commons tract 411 example](https://datacommons.org/place/geoId/51760041100); [Census Reporter tract 204](https://censusreporter.org/profiles/14000US51760020400-census-tract-204-richmond-va)).

[Richmond Public Schools](https://www.rvaschools.net/about/rps) are about 60 percent Black, 10 percent white, and 25 percent Hispanic, while many West End families use private, parochial, or Henrico schools across the city line. Life expectancy has been reported to vary by more than 20 years between ZIP codes, with the lowest figures concentrated east of I-95 and I-64, mapped by City Health Dashboard and Virginia Department of Health and relayed in local reporting ([VPM](https://www.vpm.org/news/2025-07-11/virginia-impacts-confederate-monuments-trump-stoney-martin-valentine)). HOME of VA materials state that the median Black household in Richmond holds roughly one-eighth the wealth of the median white household, mirroring HOLC-era geography ([HOME of VA booklet](https://homeofva.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/06/RIR_Booklet_HUD_10_26_18_Web.pdf)).

Qualification: the life-expectancy figure arrives via secondary news citation; this packet does not reproduce the underlying ZIP table.

### Finding 4 — Persistence is structural, not only individual choice (moderate, causal; partly synthesis)

Three drivers are named: (1) school-related geographic sorting — West End families can exit to Henrico, Chesterfield, or Goochland; East End families generally cannot; (2) suburban tax-base capture of city-linked economic activity; (3) a West End civic narrative centered on property values, schools, and safety that frames inner-city residents as problems to contain rather than co-stakeholders.

School exit options and county borders are observable facts of the metro map. The tax-capture and civic-narrative pieces are research synthesis without a dollar audit or attitude survey in this source base — treat them as hypotheses with institutional plausibility, not measured effects.

### Finding 5 — Five paired policy avenues, with contact and housing evidence stronger than narrative-alone (moderate, normative / predictive)

| Avenue | Core idea | Strongest cited base | Main limit |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Narrative reframing | Teach shared city history via museums, markers, curricula | [Valentine Race, Space and Power](https://thevalentine.org/explore/exhibitions/online-exhibitions/racial-equity-health-series/race-space-and-power-in-richmond-virginia/); Monument Avenue changes ([Fairfield Foundation](https://fairfieldfoundation.org/witness-to-change-the-rise-and-fall-of-monument-avenues-confederate-statues)) | Behavior change from narrative alone is mixed |
| Sustained contact | Equal-status, repeated, goal-linked interaction | Allport; Pettigrew & Tropp 2006 meta-analysis (515 studies); [Urban Institute mixed-income review](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/27116/412292-Effects-from-Living-in-Mixed-Income-Communities-for-Low-Income-Families.PDF) | Can become performative without shared goals |
| Anchor institutions | Hiring, procurement, community benefit | UR [Bonner Center](https://engage.richmond.edu/); VCU Health footprint; Harkavy/Hodges framework (synthesis) | Slow; leadership turnover resets priorities |
| Shared-risk policy | Inclusionary zoning, school-funding equity, anti-displacement redevelopment | [Mayor's housing plan](https://rva.gov/mayors-office/more-homes-more-people); [Purpose Built model](https://purposebuiltcommunities.org/what-we-do/our-model/) | Developer/state opposition; Gilpin plan already failed once |
| Democratic reform | Shared ballot timing, revenue authority, participatory budgeting | Local majority-nonwhite electorate; state preemption constraints | General Assembly limits; revenue-authority claim thinly sourced |

Contact evidence is the most formal social science in the packet (meta-analysis of prejudice reduction). Transfer to West End support for costly shared-risk policies is an inference, not a Richmond measurement. Narrative change without structural change is explicitly flagged as insufficient: monument removals and school renamings did not rewrite opportunity geography.

### Finding 6 — Live Richmond vehicles, including a housing-plan / Gilpin Court tension (strong on events; moderate on projections)

Mayor Avula's January 2026 [More Homes for More People](https://rva.gov/mayors-office/more-homes-more-people) plan commits about $13.4M city and ~$12.6M federal funds in FY27 toward 1,000 affordable homes, recapitalizes the Affordable Housing Trust Fund at $10M/year, and pairs a Code Refresh zoning overhaul the administration says could enable more than 30,000 new homes by right ([WRIC plan coverage](https://www.wric.com/news/local-news/richmond/mayor-avula-to-release-affordable-housing-plan-for-richmond-in-january)). The 30,000 figure is a policy projection, not built units.

In December 2025 the mayor stated he could not support the then-current RRHA Gilpin Court redevelopment plan, citing displacement scale and replacement-housing pace ([CBS 6 post](https://www.facebook.com/CBS6News/posts/richmond-mayor-danny-avula-said-he-cannot-support-the-current-plan-to-redevelop-/1337485221297352)). That clash is evidence that mixed-income redevelopment without credible anti-displacement design faces both resident and mayoral resistance — even when "change" is the shared goal.

Existing conduits include [HOME of VA](http://homeofva.org/), [Peter Paul Development Center](https://peterpaul.org/), Boaz & Ruth, the [UR Bonner Center](https://engage.richmond.edu/), VCU / VCU Health, and the Maggie Walker Community Land Trust — plus city boards where West End residents can be recruited into standing East End conversations.

## Uncertainties and competing interpretations

1. **Contact → policy support?** Pettigrew & Tropp support prejudice reduction under structured conditions; whether Bonner placements or civic partnerships increase West End votes or dollars for inclusionary zoning and school-funding equity is unmeasured in Richmond.

2. **Mixed-income as remedy or displacement vector?** The [Urban Institute review](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/27116/412292-Effects-from-Living-in-Mixed-Income-Communities-for-Low-Income-Families.PDF) finds modest gains for low-income residents and no consistent harm to higher-income neighbors when developments are well managed. Community organizers and some think tanks warn that without anti-displacement locks, redevelopment becomes a capital vehicle against current residents. The 2025 Gilpin Court rejection is a local instance of that conflict, not a settled design.

3. **Narrative without structure.** Richmond removed Confederate monuments and renamed schools; opportunity geography changed little. Narrative may be necessary for policy durability and insufficient as a substitute.

4. **Missing measurement.** No West End attitude survey; no dollar estimate of county tax capture; little direct Gilpin resident testimony in the research base; life-expectancy and some income figures need primary-table verification.

5. **Coalition durability.** Whether Mayor Avula and partners can sustain multi-year reforms past predictable backlash remains an open political question, not a research finding.

Local checks a student can run without new funding: walk Monument Avenue and an East End corridor on the same day; pull RRHA Gilpin materials against the mayor's December 2025 statements; ask [Peter Paul](https://peterpaul.org/), [HOME of VA](http://homeofva.org/), or the Maggie Walker land trust how West End volunteers actually show up; map ACS income and City Health Dashboard life expectancy for two West and two East ZIP/tract pairs; attend a West End civic meeting when Code Refresh is on the agenda and note whether East End housing is discussed in the same room.
