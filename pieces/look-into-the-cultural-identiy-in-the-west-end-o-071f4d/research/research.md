# Cultural Identity in Richmond's West End and Policy Avenues for Mobilizing Care for the Inner City

*Prepared July 2026 - Richmond, Virginia*

---

## Executive Summary

Richmond, Virginia is one of the most spatially polarized cities of its size in the United States. The West End - a term locals use loosely for the city's affluent western corridor encompassing the Fan, Museum District, Carytown, Scott's Addition, the Near West End, and the Westhampton/Chesterfield-bordering estates - is majority-white, college-educated, and wealthy. The East End and South Side - historically Black neighborhoods such as Jackson Ward, Gilpin Court, Highland Park, Whitcomb Court, Creighton Court, Hillside Court, and Mosby Court - are disproportionately Black, low-income, and have carried the cumulative weight of redlining, urban renewal, and public-housing displacement.

The cultural identity of the West End is real, but it is not monolithic: it is a layered mix of historic preservation pride (Monument Avenue's leafy boulevards), creative-economy vitality (Carytown, Scott's Addition breweries), university presence (University of Richmond), and intentional geographic separation from the city's Black urban core.

Policymakers seeking to change "hearts and minds" - to redirect West End civic energy toward the inner city - have at least five evidence-backed avenues: (1) place-based narrative reframing tied to shared city history; (2) sustained cross-class contact through schools, mixed-income housing, and service programs; (3) anchor-institution partnerships (University of Richmond, VCU, Bon Secours); (4) zoning and tax policy that put West End homeowners inside the same risk pool as East End renters; and (5) democratic reform that ties the two halves of the city into one electorate.

The biggest open question is whether Richmond's current leadership, including Mayor Danny Avula, can sustain a multi-year coalition capable of pushing these reforms past the predictable backlash that has already killed one signature public-housing redevelopment plan (the rejected Gilpin Court proposal in 2025).

## 1. What "the West End" Means in Richmond

### 1.1 Geography and Identity

The phrase "West End of Richmond" is not a single neighborhood; it is an umbrella term. Locals typically use it to refer to several overlapping areas:

- **The Fan and Museum District** - late-19th- and early-20th-century streetcar suburbs with preserved brownstones, anchored by Virginia Commonwealth University (VCU). Median household income in the Fan is roughly $70,000-$90,000 and the population is majority white (Wikipedia, "West End (Richmond, Virginia)"); [19]).
- **Carytown and Scott's Addition** - retail and creative-industrial corridors that transformed from early-20th-century streetcar stops into boutique, brewery, and tech-office districts (Wikipedia, "West End (Richmond, Virginia)")).
- **Near West End / Westhampton** - the wealthiest single-family-home enclave in the city, with home prices routinely above $700,000 and median household incomes above $120,000 ([19]).
- **University of Richmond** - a private, predominantly white institution of about 4,000 undergraduates whose 430-acre campus forms a physical and psychological border between the Near West End and the city's North Side ([68]).

A 19th-century origin matters: the Westham area (chartered in 1752) sat on the west bank of the James, and the Westham Plank Road (1804) linked Richmond to Goochland County's farms, presaging the West End's role as a westward extension of the white downtown ([19]).

### 1.2 The Symbolic Geography: Monument Avenue

The West End's cultural identity is inseparable from Monument Avenue, the grand residential boulevard originally planned as a Confederate memorial corridor and home to statues of Robert E. Lee, J.E.B. Stuart, Stonewall Jackson, Jefferson Davis, and Matthew Fontaine Maury. After George Floyd's murder in 2020, all of the city's Monument Avenue statues were removed, and in 2021 Governor Ralph Northam's administration unveiled a new monument to tennis star and Richmond native Arthur Ashe - the first statue on the avenue honoring a Black Virginian ([34]). The avenue is now contested ground: in 2025 local debates continue over how to teach and memorialize its history ([35]).

### 1.3 The Demographic Profile

- Citywide, Richmond is approximately 41 percent white, 41 percent Black, and 16 percent Hispanic or Latino (U.S. Census, Richmond city 2020 Decennial; see also [71]).
- Median household income in the city was about $64,587 (2019-2023 ACS), with a poverty rate near 18 percent ([71]). Census tract 204 (East End / Highland Park) and similar tracts lag the citywide median by tens of thousands of dollars, while West End tracts (e.g., Westhampton) run two to three times higher ([70]).
- Richmond Public Schools are 60 percent Black, 10 percent white, and 25 percent Hispanic, while many West End families send children to private or parochial schools or to Henrico County schools just across the city line ([58]).
- Life expectancy in Richmond has been measured to vary by more than 20 years between ZIP codes, a gap mapped and publicized by the City Health Dashboard and Virginia Department of Health ([35]).

## 2. Richmond's Inner City: The Other Half

### 2.1 Jackson Ward and the Long Arc of Disinvestment

Jackson Ward, just north of downtown, was the historic center of Black Richmond and was known as the "Harlem of the South" before mid-century urban renewal. After World War II, federal highway planners routed Interstate 95 directly through the neighborhood, displacing more than 7,000 residents - roughly 10 percent of Richmond's Black population at the time - and razing hundreds of Black-owned homes and businesses ([15]; [7]).

In the 1930s, the Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) graded most Black neighborhoods "D - Hazardous" (red) and the white West End "A - Best" or "B - Still Desirable" (blue/green). Federal mortgage insurance largely followed these maps, channeling decades of investment away from the inner city ([10]; [8]).

### 2.2 Public Housing and the Six Redevelopments

Richmond's six original public housing developments - Gilpin Court, Hillside Court, Creighton Court, Whitcomb Court, Mosby Court, and Highland Park (Fairfield) - were built between 1942 and 1961 to concentrate Black residents displaced by slum clearance. Together they house a population whose poverty rate is several times the citywide average ([62]).

In December 2025, Mayor Avula publicly stated he could not support the current RRHA-led plan to redevelop Gilpin Court - one of the largest concentrations of public housing in the city - citing concerns about the scale of displacement and the pace of replacement housing (WRIC 8News, "Richmond Mayor Danny Avula said he cannot support the current plan to redevelop..."). The clash crystallized the central tension: residents and the mayor want change; they disagree on whether the current plan preserves community or accelerates displacement.

### 2.3 Concrete Disparities Today

- **Health**: Life expectancy varies by more than 20 years between Richmond ZIP codes, with the lowest figures concentrated east of I-95 and I-64 ([35]).
- **Wealth**: The median Black household in Richmond holds roughly one-eighth the wealth of the median white household, mirroring the HOLC-era geography ([10]).
- **Schools**: RPS accreditation has been a moving target for two decades; chronic-absenteeism and third-grade reading proficiency lag Henrico and Chesterfield on the West End ([58]).

## 3. Why the Gap Persists - and Who Benefits

The persistence of the disparity is not an accident of individual choice. Three structural drivers keep it in place:

1. **Geographic sorting by school zoning.** Richmond is surrounded by Henrico, Chesterfield, and Goochland counties, all of which are wealthier, whiter, and have stronger schools. Any West End family that wants to leave RPS can cross a city line; East End families cannot. The city line is itself a policy artifact.
2. **Tax-base capture by counties.** West End residents who work in the city but live in the suburbs generate retail and income-tax revenue that flows to counties, not to RPS or to Richmond's affordable-housing trust fund.
3. **Civic narrative.** The dominant West End story is one of property values, schools, and safety - a vocabulary that tends to *exclude* inner-city residents as fellow stakeholders and frame them as problems to be contained.

## 4. Five Policy Avenues for Changing Hearts and Minds

The phrase "change hearts and minds" risks sounding soft. The evidence below shows that durable change in segregated cities requires *both* a moral case and a structural one - narrative without policy is symbolic, and policy without narrative is fragile.

### 4.1 Place-Based Narrative Reframing

**What it is.** A long-term, deliberate reframing of "inner city" as "shared city history" - using monuments, museums, school curricula, and city communications.

**Evidence base.** Richmond already has the raw material:
- The Valentine Museum's "Race, Space and Power" online exhibition explicitly documents how redlining, urban renewal, and highway construction created today's geography of inequity ([15]).
- The 2020 removal of the Confederate statues and the 2021 installation of the Arthur Ashe monument on the same avenue created a physical anchor for that reframing ([34]).
- Virginia's 2022 budget included funds for the Maggie L. Walker National Historic Site interpretive upgrades ([The Valentine / NPS partnerships]).

**What policymakers can do.**
- Fund a "Richmond Story" civic curriculum required in all RPS middle schools and made available to Henrico and Chesterfield schools.
- Require city-funded capital projects (parks, libraries, transit stations) to carry interpretive markers that connect the site to Richmond's racial history.
- Use the Mayor's communications platform to repeat, in plain language, that the East End is *not* "their" problem but the city's problem.

**Limits / open questions.** Empirical research on whether narrative reframing alone changes behavior is mixed; it is strongest when paired with concrete contact and accountability (see 4.2 below).

### 4.2 Sustained Cross-Class Contact

**What it is.** Structured, repeated, equal-status interaction across class and race - schools, civic programs, mixed-income housing, service projects.

**Evidence base.** The "contact hypothesis" (Gordon Allport, 1954; updated by Thomas Pettigrew and Linda Tropp's 2006 meta-analysis of 515 studies, n > 250,000) shows that well-structured intergroup contact reliably reduces prejudice; the effect is larger when participants have repeated, cooperative, equal-status interaction rather than one-off encounters. Practical programs include:
- **Mixed-income housing.** The Urban Institute's review of mixed-income developments finds modest but real improvements in adult outcomes for low-income residents (employment, health, safety) and *no consistent negative effects* on higher-income residents when the development is well-managed ([25]).
- **School integration.** Century Foundation research and the Harvard-based Equality of Opportunity Project find that low-income students who attend integrated schools do significantly better on long-term measures including college attendance and adult income.

**What Richmond can do.**
- Pilot a "Cross-Richmond Schools" program in which West End private schools and parochial schools reserve a defined percentage of need-based seats for RPS students, funded by an auxiliary scholarship.
- Make mixed-income housing a default at any site receiving city subsidy (this is now standard practice under Mayor Avula's housing plan, see 4.5).
- Support *voluntary*, facilitated dialogue programs that pair West End civic associations with East End neighborhood groups around specific, time-bound projects (a community garden, a school-pantry partnership) - not vague "conversations."

**Limits.** Critics (e.g., Rich Elmore and Jelani Cobb) note that contact programs can become performative; the key is shared *goals*, not just shared *space*.

### 4.3 Anchor Institutions as Conduits

**What it is.** Universities, hospitals, and major employers use their hiring, purchasing, and real-estate footprint to lift - or entrench - neighborhood conditions.

**Evidence base.** The "anchor institution" framework (Harkavy, Hodges, and colleagues at the University of Pennsylvania) holds that institutions of place (universities, hospitals, civic organizations) can deliberately align their routine operations with neighborhood revitalization. UR's Bonner Center for Civic Engagement already places students in East End partner sites; VCU has a substantial East End footprint through VCU Health.

**What Richmond can do.**
- Expand "local hire" provisions in city contracting so that publicly subsidized construction on the West End or citywide draws from East End zip codes (this is standard in many federal community benefit agreements).
- Use University of Richmond, VCU, and Virginia Union University as conveners of a joint "Richmond Civic Equity Compact" that benchmarks each institution's local hiring, local procurement, and place-based investment.
- Encourage Bon Secours and VCU Health to direct a defined share of community benefit spending to East End primary care and behavioral health, where capacity gaps are sharpest.

**Limits.** Anchor institution work is slow; it depends on leadership turnover that can reset priorities. Quantifying the impact on citywide attitudes is harder than counting construction jobs.

### 4.4 Policy That Forces Shared Geography

**Why it matters.** "Hearts and minds" campaigns that run ahead of structural change are fragile. The most durable changes in mixed cities have come when policy puts West End households and East End households into the same risk pools.

**Concrete levers.**
- **Inclusionary zoning.** Require any new residential development of a defined size in the West End (and citywide) to set aside a share of units as affordable. (Virginia's 2021-2022 state-level debate over preemption shows this is contested but feasible; Richmond has begun the conversation as part of Mayor Avula's "Code Refresh" housing plan, which the administration describes as enabling more than 30,000 new homes by right through zoning reform - see City of Richmond, Mayor's Plan for Affordable Housing.)
- **School funding equity.** Use a city-and-county revenue pool, a transfer from suburban counties, or a state formula adjustment so that RPS per-pupil funding rises toward suburban levels.
- **Mixed-income public-housing redevelopment.** Move beyond the failed 2025 Gilpin Court plan toward a Purpose Built Communities model that keeps existing residents in place, wraps mixed-income housing around a cradle-to-college education pipeline and community health facilities ([84]).

**Limits.** Each lever faces entrenched opposition. Inclusionary zoning in particular draws resistance from developers; school funding formulas require state legislative action; and public-housing redevelopment has just failed once in Richmond, so the next iteration will be harder to launch.

### 4.5 Democratic and Civic Reform

**What it is.** The simplest structural change is to make sure that West End political power and East End political power share the same ballot and the same coalition.

**Evidence base.** Richmond is a majority-nonwhite city whose recent mayors (Stoney, Avula) and city council reflect that majority. State-level preemption, however, constrains the city's ability to raise revenue and zone independently. Reforms that would matter:
- Restore some degree of revenue-raising authority to Richmond (currently more constrained than any other Virginia city of comparable size).
- Move city elections to even-year, higher-turnout cycles so that West End voters and East End voters both show up.
- Expand participatory-budgeting and neighborhood-deliberation processes that have residents of both halves co-author priorities.

**Limits.** State-level politics constrains Richmond's autonomy. Local reformers can only push so hard against the General Assembly.

## 5. Vehicles Already in Motion in Richmond

The policy avenues above are not abstract. Several concrete vehicles exist:

- **Mayor Danny Avula's "More Homes for More People" plan** (released January 2026) commits $13.4M of city funds and ~$12.6M of federal funds in FY27 to preserve or create 1,000 affordable homes, recapitalizes the Affordable Housing Trust Fund at $10M/year, and pairs a "Code Refresh" zoning overhaul that the administration says will enable more than 30,000 new homes by right (City of Richmond, Mayor's Office; [4]).
- **Housing Opportunities Made Equal of Virginia (HOME of VA)** is a regional fair-housing organization that conducts audits, enforcement, and education on housing discrimination ([3]).
- **Peter Paul Development Center** (1708 N. 22nd St., East End), a ministry of the Episcopal Diocese of Virginia, has run youth, senior, and food programs for East End families since 1979 ([38]; [42]).
- **Boaz & Ruth** is a Richmond nonprofit that blends workforce training, affordable housing, and small-business incubation in the inner city (Boaz & Ruth, via Facebook).
- **University of Richmond Bonner Center for Civic Engagement** runs the Bonner Scholars program, which places hundreds of UR undergraduates in sustained East End and South Side community partnerships ([68]).
- **VCU and VCU Health** are anchor institutions with deep East End physical and economic footprints (e.g., VCU Massey Comprehensive Cancer Center, the planned Arts District expansion).
- **The Maggie L. Walker Initiative / Maggie Walker Community Land Trust** works on permanent affordability in neighborhoods like Jackson Ward and Randolph.
- **Boards and commissions**: the Richmond Planning Commission, the Affordable Housing Trust Fund Advisory Board, and the new Resilience and Sustainability Committee are all venues where West End residents can be recruited into East End conversations with standing.

## 6. The Counter-Evidence and Open Questions

Three tensions are unresolved in the available evidence:

1. **Does contact change hearts?** Pettigrew's meta-analysis supports it; more recent work (e.g., on "peace walls" in Belfast, on charter-school integration) suggests contact matters most when participants have a *common goal* and *equal status*. Pure social mixing without shared work does less.
2. **Is mixed-income housing a Trojan horse for displacement?** Critics, including the right-leaning Manhattan Institute and left-leaning community organizers, both warn that without anti-displacement locks, mixed-income redevelopment becomes a vector for private capital to push out the very residents it claims to serve. The Avula administration's Gilpin Court rejection in December 2025 is a live example.
3. **Can narrative reframing outrun structural forces?** Richmond's recent history is a mixed ledger: the city removed Confederate monuments and renamed J.E.B. Stuart Elementary as Barack Obama Elementary, yet the underlying geography of opportunity changed little. Narrative is a precondition for policy durability, but not a substitute.

---

## 7. A Practical Agenda for the Next Two Years

A workable near-term agenda would combine the four vehicles above with three moves specifically designed to engage West End residents:

- **Anchor a "Richmond Civic Equity Compact"** with UR, VCU, VUU, Bon Secours, and VCU Health - each committing to defined East End local hiring, procurement, and place-based investment targets published annually.
- **Tie West End homeowners to East End investment** by dedicating a percentage of every new City affordable-housing dollar to the same East End census tracts most affected by historic HOLC D-grades, so West End taxpayers see a tangible return for their contributions.
- **Use the Avula housing plan's "Code Refresh"** as the bridge: every West End civic association invited into the zoning-reform conversation is also invited into the East End affordable-housing conversation. The same meetings discuss both, with the same data and the same staff.
- **Recruit the West End's institutions** - UR, private schools, country clubs, churches, civic leagues - into "cross-city service partnerships" with named East End anchors (Peter Paul Development Center, Boaz & Ruth, the Maggie Walker Land Trust), built around recurring projects (school mentoring, small-business support, food access) rather than one-off volunteering.

---

## 8. Closing

Richmond's West End is a real place with a real identity - a leafy, museum-rich, increasingly walkable corridor whose residents have disproportionate political and philanthropic power. The inner city is not a separate jurisdiction but the same city, and the West End's identity and the East End's distress are two faces of the same historical ledger. Policymakers who want to change "hearts and minds" should not treat these as separate problems: they are one problem, viewed from two zip codes. The evidence shows that durable change requires narrative, contact, anchor-institution leverage, policy that forces shared geography, and democratic reform - applied together, over years, with accountability.
