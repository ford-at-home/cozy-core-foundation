# STORYTELLING.md — Argument Construction

The voice files (`STYLE.md`, `ANTI-SLOP.md`) tell you what each sentence should sound like. This file tells you how the **whole piece** should be built.

The philosophy is Dave Chappelle's, lifted out of comedy. Most of his bits work because he sets up casually, lets the audience think they know the target, then moves it underneath them. He's not mainly a joke-joke-joke comic. He's a _story_ comic who uses jokes to smuggle in arguments. The sentence-level moves in `STYLE.md` are the texture; this is the architecture above the texture.

When the piece isn't comedy, the architecture still works. An essay, a manifesto, a hard email, a long-form post all face the same problem: the reader didn't ask to be argued with, and the moment you announce your thesis you've lost them.

---

## The default question

Don't ask:

> "what is true about this situation?"

That produces an explainer. Explainers are fine. The world has plenty of them.

Ask instead:

> "what is the lie everyone is telling about this situation?"

That produces a piece. The gap between the public posture and the private knowledge is where the work lives. If you can't name a gap, you might not have a piece yet — you might just have a topic.

---

## The 15 mechanics

Each mechanic is Chappelle's. Each translation is what it looks like when the medium is prose, not standup.

### 1. Start friendly so you can go somewhere dangerous

Open in conversational mode. A reader who thinks they're hearing a story listens. A reader who hears a thesis statement prepares to disagree.

**In prose:** Don't open with the claim. Open with a scene, a weird encounter, a small concrete event. The argument walks in casually behind it. By the time the reader notices what the piece is actually about, they're already inside.

### 2. Make the reader think they know the target

Set up an obvious target. Let the reader's instincts fire. Then move it.

**In prose:** The piece that looks like a critique of remote work for the first three paragraphs is actually about middle managers. The post that looks like founder advice is actually about VC incentives. The bait isn't dishonest — the obvious target is real. It just isn't the _whole_ target.

### 3. Use taboo as fuel, not decoration

A weak comic uses taboo like a firecracker: laugh because the thing was offensive. That gets old. Chappelle uses taboo because taboo _marks where the social pressure is_, and the pressure is the subject.

**In prose:** When you find yourself wanting to soften a sentence, _that's_ the sentence. The hesitation marks the actual claim worth making. Don't write the softened version. Write into the hesitation and find out why it's there.

### 4. Build scenes, not just punchlines

Chappelle's stories work because you can picture them: the place, the type of people in the room, the social rule being enforced, the thing nobody admits, the moment someone breaks it.

**In prose:** Every abstract claim should have a moment underneath it where a specific person did or said a specific thing. "Engineers undervalue documentation" is air. "I watched a staff engineer ship a PR with a 14-word commit message that broke prod for the new hire on Monday" is a scene. Air doesn't argue. Scenes do.

This is the architectural form of `STYLE.md`'s _always name the specific_. The signature move handles the sentence; this handles the paragraph and the section.

### 5. Play dumb before playing smart

Chappelle opens with the blunt, slightly-undignified version of the thought, then sharpens it. If he starts too smart, the bit becomes a lecture.

**In prose:** If you start at maximum sophistication, the piece reads as a lecture. Open closer to where your reader already lives — even if it makes you look a little simple — and let the sophistication arrive over the next few paragraphs. The reader stays with you because you stayed with them.

### 6. Make yourself part of the mess

Chappelle's strongest stories don't make him look noble. He's scared, selfish, lustful, hypocritical, impressed by power. He's inside the same machine as everyone else.

**In prose:** When you criticize a pattern, name where you do it yourself. The piece about LinkedIn slop should admit which of your own posts have it. The piece about over-engineering should admit which of your codebases shows it. This is the `STYLE.md` _self-deprecating reframe_ move, sharpened: it's not a hedge, it's an _epistemic stance_. A writer who stands outside the situation as moral judge is doing propaganda. A writer who stands inside it is doing storytelling.

### 7. Control when the reader is allowed to relax

Easy laugh, bigger laugh, dangerous turn, nervous laugh, pause, harder truth, release, callback, final reversal. That's the Chappelle rhythm.

**In prose:** The _long-short-short-long cadence_ in `STYLE.md` is the surface mechanic. The deeper one is _when the reader is allowed to nod along and when they're required to sit with discomfort_. A piece with no nervous pauses is either trivial or dishonest. If every paragraph is comfortable, you haven't said anything yet.

### 8. Tell stories like legal arguments

Facts, context, motive, contradiction, conclusion. But shaped like a person talking, not a lawyer reading.

**In prose:** The piece should be doing three or four things at once underneath — proving a point, complicating a villain, exposing hypocrisy, showing how power actually works — without the structure announcing itself. The reader should feel they're following a story. You should know you're building a case.

### 9. Use blunt language to keep the argument from getting precious

Chappelle drops vulgar or street-level phrasing right when the idea threatens to levitate into theory. High-level idea, low-level phrasing. The contrast keeps the bit alive.

**In prose:** When the writing starts to sound like "the discourse," reach for a physical, blunt word. This is not a license for vulgarity for its own sake — it's a tool for puncturing your own pretension when you feel it rising. The moment a sentence starts to feel like it could appear in someone's TED talk, kill it.

### 10. Treat group identity as a social operating system

Chappelle's race material isn't "white people do X, Black people do Y." It's: _you all know the rules. stop pretending you don't._

**In prose:** When writing about any social category — engineers, founders, parents, Richmond locals, anybody — the real material is rarely the category itself. It's the _invisible etiquette around the category_: who's allowed to criticize them, who's allowed to praise them, who gets called naïve for either, who gets believed, who gets watched, who gets treated as an individual, who gets turned into a symbol. The piece lives in the etiquette, not the category.

### 11. Use the reader as part of the bit

Chappelle plays the audience as an instrument. If they laugh too hard, that becomes part of the story. If they get quiet, same thing.

**In prose:** Anticipate the reader's reaction inside the piece. "You probably winced at that last sentence. Good. Here's why I wrote it." This pulls the reader into the construction. They're not consuming the argument anymore — they're implicated in it. The `STYLE.md` _name the critique your move is vulnerable to_ signature is one specific form of this move.

### 12. Callbacks that mean something worse the second time

A normal callback says: _remember that earlier joke?_ Chappelle's better callbacks say: _remember that earlier joke? it means something worse now._

**In prose:** The image, phrase, or scene you used in paragraph 2 should come back in paragraph 8 carrying everything that happened in between. By paragraph 14, it should be the whole point. This isn't reusing a metaphor — it's _compounding_ one.

### 13. End with a blunt line that collapses the whole piece

Chappelle's endings reframe everything before them in one sentence. Not a moral. Not a summary. A reframe.

**In prose:** `STYLE.md` says closers are short, direct, low-stakes, never restate the thesis. The Chappelle version sharpens that: the closer should _change what the piece was about_. The reader thought they were reading X. The closer reveals they were reading Y all along. The pieces that linger are the ones where the closer makes you re-read the title.

### 14. The basic shape

The whole engine, reduced:

1. **Casual entry.** Scene, encounter, observation. Not the thesis.
2. **Surface the social rule.** The thing everyone is operating under.
3. **Show public obedience.** How people perform their respect for the rule.
4. **Name the private truth.** What everyone actually knows underneath.
5. **Let the reader feel the recognition.** The wince, the laugh, the "oh shit." This is the prose equivalent of the audience laugh.
6. **Complicate the easy target.** The first villain isn't the only villain.
7. **Reveal the real power dynamic.** Who actually benefits from the rule staying in place.
8. **Implicate yourself, the reader, or the institution.** The piece doesn't stand outside.
9. **End with the blunt reframing line.** One sentence that re-organizes the rest.

Not every piece needs all nine. The minimum to be doing this at all is: casual entry, named gap between public and private, implicated self or reader, reframing closer.

### 15. Don't ask what's true; ask what's the lie

Same as the default question at the top of this file. Repeated here because it's the one mechanic to remember if you forget the other fourteen.

## How this composes with `STYLE.md`

The sentence-level moves in `STYLE.md` are already doing some of this work. Naming the overlap so you can see where the architecture wires into the texture:

| `STYLE.md` move                                    | `STORYTELLING.md` mechanic it executes      |
| -------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Paragraph openings ("Stay with me for a second")   | #1 (start friendly), #5 (play dumb first)   |
| Long-short-short-long cadence                      | #7 (tension control)                        |
| Self-deprecating reframe / confessional disclosure | #6 (part of the mess), #11 (use the reader) |
| Always name the specific                           | #4 (scenes, not assertions)                 |
| Conversational headings                            | #1 (friendly entry), #5 (play dumb)         |
| Closers (never restate the thesis)                 | #13 (blunt reframing line)                  |
| Anaphoric rhythm                                   | #12 (compounding callbacks)                 |

If you've never read `STORYTELLING.md` but you've been writing per `STYLE.md`, you've probably been doing four or five of these mechanics without naming them. The point of this file is to name them so the other nine stop being accidents.

## When this is the wrong philosophy

Don't apply this to:

- **Operational comms.** Anything that should load `KSP.md`. Status updates, bug reports, PR descriptions, on-call handoffs, Slack messages. State the signal; don't architect an argument.
- **Reference docs.** API docs, runbooks, schemas — anything where the reader is _trying to look something up_. The Chappelle move is for pieces the reader chose to read. A docs page is one someone was forced to find.
- **Acknowledgments, condolences, thank-yous.** The setup-and-reframe move misreads the room. Just say the thing.

If the piece is something a reader opted into and the subject has a public-private gap, this file applies. Otherwise: KSP.
